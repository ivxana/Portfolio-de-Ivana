Competitions.

Where I learned to think on my feet.

Overview

Competitive case competitions and clubs that taught me how to argue, sell, and think under pressure.

Why This Matters

Most students choose one. I chose three.

DECA teaches sales. Case comps teach structured thinking. Mock Trial teaches argumentation. You need all three to actually move ideas through the world. These aren’t extracurriculars - they’re training grounds for the skills I use every time I cold-email an expert or pitch a project.

02

Mock Trial

Argumentation

Lawyer - Cross Examination

I almost didn’t join. I wanted more team activities and chances to make friends, and I knew I enjoyed law. My law teacher convinced me over weeks, making every accommodation to get me to say yes. And I almost quit twice - terrified of juggling too many things and doing none of them well.

But someone believed in me before I believed in myself. So I said yes.

Practices energized me. At first I focused on my own role - I told myself to handle what I could. I underestimated my teammates. Competition day? Our team killed it.

That feeling when I’d walk away from the stand after crushing my cross examination was one I hadn’t felt in a long time. The pride that comes from knowing there’s a team ready to high five you the second you turn around is unbeatable.

4th Final placement - missed the podium by a tiny margin
What Mock Trial taught me
  • How to find flaws in witnesses’ stories in real time - cross examination as a live skill
  • How to care about the team’s performance, not just my own
  • Sometimes there’s more joy in watching others grow than in winning yourself

Reflection

None of these competitions are the work. But the skills they teach - fast research, clear communication, defending your reasoning to skeptical judges - are exactly what I use every time I cold-email an expert or pitch a project.
