BioVerify

Early Flu Detection for Schools

Overview

BioVerify explored how schools could identify potential flu outbreaks earlier through environmental monitoring and AI-assisted verification systems.

Built in two hours during a TKS hackathon - and won. The project asked a deceptively simple question: what if schools could detect what’s in the air before it shows up in absence records?

TYPE Hackathon Build
FOCUS Biotech · AI · Public Health
STATUS Concept & Prototype

Why It Matters

Flu doesn’t wait for absence records.

Traditional flu surveillance is reactive. By the time an outbreak is visible in attendance data, it’s already spreading. Schools have dense, contained populations - exactly the conditions where early detection matters most and where it’s historically been the hardest to act on quickly.

BioVerify started from a different premise: what if we could detect environmental signals before symptoms surface?

The Concept

Environmental sensing meets AI verification.

The system would continuously sample classroom air for influenza markers. Those signals would be processed, cross-checked against contextual data - absence trends, seasonal patterns, confidence thresholds - and only then surface as verified alerts to school administrators.

The goal wasn’t just detection. It was detection that was trustworthy enough to act on.

Focus Areas

01

Proactive Health Infrastructure

Shifting schools from reactive outbreak management to continuous environmental monitoring that surfaces signals early.

02

AI Verification Design

Building verification layers to reduce noise - so alerts that reach decision-makers are trustworthy enough to act on.

03

Accessible Deployment

Designing for low-cost, scalable integration into existing school environments - not purpose-built facilities.

What I Explored

Systems design from the signal out.

My focus was the AI layer: how do you design a verification system that filters environmental noise, contextualizes signals, and produces alerts that are actually useful to someone who has to make a decision in real time?

I also worked on how the biological sensing layer and the institutional response layer would talk to each other - the handoff from detection to action.

System Thinking

Detection alone is not enough.

For a detection system to be useful in practice, the information it surfaces must be understandable, actionable, timely, and trusted.

That realization shaped how BioVerify was designed. A detection system that produces noisy, hard-to-interpret, or poorly-timed alerts won’t get used. The gap between technically detecting something and operationally acting on it is where most of these systems fall apart.

The design challenge was as much communication as it was engineering.

Potential Impact

Earlier signals. Fewer lost days.

  • Earlier outbreak detection before symptoms become visible in attendance
  • Faster institutional response - cleaning, communication, isolation protocols
  • Fewer preventable transmission chains across classrooms
  • Lower strain on healthcare systems during seasonal peaks
  • A model extensible beyond flu - toward broader respiratory health monitoring

Reflection

The most interesting part wasn’t the sensor. It was the gap between detecting something and doing something about it.

BioVerify was built in two hours under hackathon conditions. What made it interesting wasn’t the speed - it was the question it forced: what does it actually take for a detection system to produce change?

The answer isn’t just technical. It involves trust, communication, institutional buy-in, and usability under pressure. Those constraints shaped everything.
