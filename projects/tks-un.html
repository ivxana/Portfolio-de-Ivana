Anticipating Conflict
Before Escalation

Using environmental pressure modelling and satellite intelligence to identify high-risk cattle convergence zones in South Sudan before violence occurs.

In South Sudan, climate volatility has disrupted traditional cattle migration routes, forcing pastoral communities into the same grazing corridors in search of water and viable pasture. As environmental pressure intensifies, competition over limited resources increasingly contributes to local conflict and humanitarian instability.

Our team explored how environmental signals could be transformed into an upstream early-warning system, enabling humanitarian organizations to identify high-risk convergence zones before violence escalates.

The Problem

Conflict wasn't the starting point.

Most conflict monitoring systems are reactive by design. They begin tracking risk after violence, displacement, or territorial tension has already emerged.

But in South Sudan, conflict often begins much earlier — through environmental pressure.

As droughts intensify, flooding blocks routes, and grazing land degrades, cattle movement becomes forced rather than voluntary. Multiple groups are then pushed toward the same habitable zones, compressing negotiation windows from months into days.

Rather than treating conflict as the primary signal, we reframed cattle movement itself as an early-warning opportunity.

Core Insight

Movement is a survival decision.

Pastoral communities do not move cattle randomly. Movement patterns are heavily influenced by:

  • Rainfall variability
  • Vegetation stress
  • Flooding exposure
  • Water accessibility
  • Environmental habitability

This means cattle migration can become partially predictable through environmental and geospatial signals. Our team explored whether environmental pressure data could help identify where movement convergence — and therefore conflict risk — was most likely to emerge before escalation occurred.

What We Built

Environmental Early-Warning System

We designed a geospatial early-warning framework that continuously monitored environmental stress indicators using open-source satellite and climate datasets. The system translated environmental pressure into ranked geographic priority zones for humanitarian coordination teams.

Data Inputs
  • CHIRPS rainfall anomaly data
  • NDVI vegetation stress analysis
  • Sentinel-1 flood exposure imagery
  • Soil moisture indicators
  • Surface water monitoring
  • Historical conflict validation layers
System Outputs
  • Tier 1 high-risk convergence zones
  • Tier 2 monitoring regions
  • Environmental pressure alerts
  • Preventative coordination windows
  • Operational deployment recommendations

Instead of predicting individual incidents, the system identified where structural pressure was concentrating risk.

Validation & Signal Testing

Identifying meaningful spatial patterns

To validate whether environmental pressure contained useful forward-looking signals, we tested whether conflict events disproportionately clustered inside environmentally habitable zones. The results suggested that violence was not spatially random.

Signal Test 1

Conflict events showed significant concentration inside environmentally identified habitable zones.

65.6% average conflict capture rate
3.99× peak lift over baseline probability
Signal Test 2

We then modelled overlap corridors where multiple movement pathways converged. These convergence zones produced even stronger conflict concentration signals.

5.75× conflict lift at peak overlap zones
5–12% of land area covered by targeted overlap zones

The project demonstrated how environmental pressure could potentially serve as an upstream indicator for preventative humanitarian coordination.

Humanitarian Framing

The goal was not certainty.
The goal was time.

The system was designed as a decision-support framework rather than an autonomous prediction engine. Earlier environmental signals could create:

  • More time for mediation
  • Earlier coordination between field teams
  • Better resource positioning
  • Preventative engagement before escalation

Even imperfect early signals can expand the window for action.
